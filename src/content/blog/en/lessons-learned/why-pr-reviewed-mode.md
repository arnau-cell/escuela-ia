When we designed the [news pipeline](/en/share/how-i-built-it/news-pipeline-n8n/), the obvious question
was: if it already generates the summary and creates the files on its own, why not publish it directly?
The answer we landed on — and one that took us a while to see clearly — is that the risk isn't where it
looks.

## The risk isn't the AI "making things up"

Each news item's summary isn't generated by a language model: it's extractive, literally the lab's own
official RSS summary, trimmed to a reasonable length. There's no hallucination possible there because
there's no generation — it's just copying and trimming text the lab already published. That wasn't the
weak point.

## The real risk: half-finished automatic translation

This site is genuinely bilingual — every Spanish page has an English counterpart, linked by a shared
identifier (`translationKey`), and a script (`i18n-check.mjs`) that **fails the entire build** if a pair
drifts out of sync without flagging it. For news, almost every source publishes in English only. With no
translation service turned on yet (a pending decision, not a technical one — it's waiting on approval of
which one to use), the system has a fallback: if there's no translation, the Spanish file is generated
with the English text as-is, flagged `translationOutdated: true`. The site then shows a visible notice
("this translation may be out of date") instead of pretending there's a real translation where there
isn't one.

That fallback is honest, but it isn't the same as a good translation. Publishing it automatically, with
nobody looking at it first, would mean the Spanish version of the site sometimes shows untranslated
English — exactly what this project exists to avoid (genuinely bilingual, not just for show).

## The decision: PR-reviewed mode, not "we'll automate it later"

That's why every news item the pipeline generates arrives as a branch and a Pull Request, not a direct
commit. Someone reads it, fixes the translation if needed, and decides whether to merge. It's slower than
full automation — on purpose. The rule we set: let it run this way for **at least 1-2 weeks** and only
then evaluate automating the merge, and only once a real translation service is actually connected, not
before.

## The lesson, in one line

When you automate something that touches the perceived quality of what you publish (here, the language
it's read in), the place to put human review isn't "wherever the AI might fail" but "wherever the system
can silently degrade without anyone noticing." The outdated-translation notice already warns the reader;
the Pull Request stops that notice from being the only line of defense.
